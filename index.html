The natural space is now surrounded by both native and invasive plant species.

The now-decomissioned basin, which held 300 million gallons of watered, helped Brooklyn boom as an industrial hub.

The reservoir is still surrounded by its original red brick 1858 gatehouses.

Endangered and threatened plant and animal species call the protected space home

There are 2.3 miles of paths, for both walking and biking, around the reservoir.

The diverse trees and vegetation provide a habitat for 148 species of birds, accodring to the New York City Audubon.

The Ridgewood Reservoir at Highland Park, which sits between Brooklyn and Queens, once provided the City of Brooklyn with water, allowing it to take off as an industrial force in the late 1800s.

Local environmental and historical groups pushed for the status of the natural space, which earned a spot on the National Register of Historic Places last year.

The reservoir and surrounding green space is home to approximately 150 bird species, such as the endangered short-eared owl.

"The Newton Historical Society is proud to have been a part of this amazing effort," the group's president Christina Wilkinson wrote in an announcement. "We believe that environmentalism and historic preservation go hand-in-hand."
